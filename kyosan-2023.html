本文（全9段落）

1Here's something that has always puzzled me, growing up in the US as a child of Russian parents. Whenever I or my friends were having our photos taken, we were told to say "cheese" and smile. But if my parents also happened to be in the photo, they were stone-faced. So were my Russian relatives, in their vacation photos. My parents' high-school graduation pictures show them with their young classmates looking absolutely depressed.

2This does not mean they are all unhappy! Quite the opposite. It's just that grinning without cause is not a skill Russians possess or even want. There's even a Russian saying that translates as "laughing for no reason is a sign of stupidity."

3Russians' fondness for the gentle frown seems very unusual to some foreigners. And the cultural difference goes both ways. Russians who move to America often remark on the new experience of being smiled at by strangers.

4So why is this? Why do some societies not encourage casual smiling? I got my answer when I came across a paper by Kuba Krys, a psychologist at the Polish Academy of Sciences. In some countries, smiling might not be a sign of warmth or even respect. It's evidence that you're a fool — a tricky fool.

5Krys focused on a country's "social stability." Countries that are low on this scale tend to have social systems like law courts and health-care that are unreliable and unstable. Therefore, people there view the future as unpredictable and uncontrollable.

6Smiling is a sign of certainty and confidence, so when people in those countries smile, it might seem odd to others around them. Why would you smile when fate is an invisible danger waiting to hurt you? You might, in those "low stability" countries, even be considered stupid for smiling.

7To test this theory, Krys had thousands of people in 44 different countries judge a series of eight smiling and non-smiling faces on a scale of honesty and intelligence. He compared their answers to each country's ranking of social stability from a 2004 study of 62 societies.

8He found that in countries like Germany and Switzerland, people with smiling faces were rated as significantly more intelligent than non-smiling people. But in India, Iran, and Russia, those with smiling faces were considered significantly less intelligent. Even after controlling for other factors, like the economy, there was a strong correlation between how unpredictable a society was and the likelihood its members would consider smiling people unintelligent.

9It's worth noting that other studies have found there might be other social factors that play a greater role in emotional expression — which smiling is certainly a part of. And there's evidence that some cultures don't value happiness very highly, which would affect how often people there force themselves to smile. Krys' work could be expanded and repeated, but it might be comforting for any happy Americans who find themselves puzzled by a non-smiling situation like on a subway in Russia.

前半｜謎の提示 [1]-[3]
Here's something that has always puzzled me, growing up in the US as a child of Russian parents. Whenever I or my friends were having our photos taken, we were told to say "cheese" and smile. But if my parents also happened to be in the photo, they were stone-faced. So were my Russian relatives, in their vacation photos. My parents' high-school graduation pictures show them with their young classmates looking absolutely depressed.
ロシア人の両親の子としてアメリカで育った私には、いつも不思議に思うことがありました。私や友だちは写真を撮ってもらうとき、いつも「チーズ」と言って笑顔になるように言われていました。ところが、たまたま両親も一緒に写っていると、彼らはむっつり顔でした。私のロシア人の親戚も、休暇の写真ではそのように写っていました。両親の高校卒業式の写真は、すっかり沈み込んだ表情の若いクラスメイトたちと一緒に写った彼らを写し出しています。
This does not mean they are all unhappy! Quite the opposite. It's just that grinning without cause is not a skill Russians possess or even want. There's even a Russian saying that translates as "laughing for no reason is a sign of stupidity."
だからといって、彼らが皆、不幸だというわけではありません！　まったく逆です。ただ、理由もなくにっこり笑うのは、ロシア人が持っている、あるいは持ちたいとさえ思うスキルではないのです。ロシアには「理由もなく笑うのは愚かさの印である」と訳されることわざさえあります。
Russians' fondness for the gentle frown seems very unusual to some foreigners. And the cultural difference goes both ways. Russians who move to America often remark on the new experience of being smiled at by strangers.
ロシア人が軽いしかめ面を好むことは、一部の外国人にはとても異常に見えます。そして、この文化的な違いは双方向で現れます。アメリカに移住するロシア人は、見知らぬ人からほほ笑みかけられるという新しい体験について、よく口にします。
ポイント｜[1]-[3]＝問7・8・9
両親は写真でむっつり・沈んだ表情（問7）。理由なく笑うのは「持たず・欲しがらないスキル」「愚かさの印」（問8）。アメリカで見知らぬ人に笑われる new experience＝驚き（問9）。frown「しかめ面」はタイトル（問13）の鍵語。
中盤｜クリスの理論 [4]-[6]
So why is this? Why do some societies not encourage casual smiling? I got my answer when I came across a paper by Kuba Krys, a psychologist at the Polish Academy of Sciences. In some countries, smiling might not be a sign of warmth or even respect. It's evidence that you're a fool — a tricky fool.
では、これはなぜなのでしょう？　なぜ、ある社会は打ち解けた笑顔を奨励しないのでしょうか？　その答えは、ポーランド科学アカデミーの心理学者、クバ・クリスの論文にたまたま出会ったときに見つかりました。ある国々では、笑顔は温かさや、敬意の印でさえないかもしれません。それは、あなたが愚か者──油断のならない愚か者──だという証拠なのです。
Krys focused on a country's "social stability." Countries that are low on this scale tend to have social systems like law courts and health-care that are unreliable and unstable. Therefore, people there view the future as unpredictable and uncontrollable.
クリスは、国の「社会的安定性」に注目しました。この尺度が低い国々は、裁判所や医療といった社会体制が信頼できず、不安定である傾向があります。そのため、そうした国の人々は、未来を予測できず、制御もできないものと見なしています。
Smiling is a sign of certainty and confidence, so when people in those countries smile, it might seem odd to others around them. Why would you smile when fate is an invisible danger waiting to hurt you? You might, in those "low stability" countries, even be considered stupid for smiling.
笑顔は確信と自信の印なので、そうした国々の人々がほほ笑むと、周りの人には奇妙に映るかもしれません。目に見えない危険である運命が、あなたを傷つけようと待ち受けているときに、なぜほほ笑むのでしょうか？　こうした「低安定」の国々では、ほほ笑むだけで愚か者だと思われることさえあるかもしれません。
ポイント｜[4]-[6]＝因果の鎖（問10）
社会的安定性が低い → 未来が予測・制御不能 → 笑顔（＝確信の印）が場違い → considered stupid for smiling「笑うと愚かと思われる」（問10）。「危険な運命」は「笑う理由がない」文脈であって、「笑う＝危険にある合図」ではない（問10の罠）。
後半｜実験・結果・補足 [7]-[9]
To test this theory, Krys had thousands of people in 44 different countries judge a series of eight smiling and non-smiling faces on a scale of honesty and intelligence. He compared their answers to each country's ranking of social stability from a 2004 study of 62 societies.
この理論を検証するために、クリスは44の異なる国々の数千人もの人々に、笑顔と笑顔ではない一連の8枚の顔写真を、正直さと知性の尺度で判断してもらいました。彼はその答えを、2004年の62の社会を対象とした研究から得られた各国の社会安定度ランキングと比較しました。
He found that in countries like Germany and Switzerland, people with smiling faces were rated as significantly more intelligent than non-smiling people. But in India, Iran, and Russia, those with smiling faces were considered significantly less intelligent. Even after controlling for other factors, like the economy, there was a strong correlation between how unpredictable a society was and the likelihood its members would consider smiling people unintelligent.
彼が明らかにしたのは、ドイツやスイスのような国々では、笑顔の人々は笑っていない人々よりも明らかに知性が高いと評価されていたということです。しかし、インド、イラン、ロシアでは、笑顔の人々は明らかに知性が低いと思われていました。経済などの他の要因を考慮に入れても、社会がどれほど予測不可能かということと、その構成員が笑顔の人々を知性が低いと見なす可能性との間には、強い相関関係があったのです。
It's worth noting that other studies have found there might be other social factors that play a greater role in emotional expression — which smiling is certainly a part of. And there's evidence that some cultures don't value happiness very highly, which would affect how often people there force themselves to smile. Krys' work could be expanded and repeated, but it might be comforting for any happy Americans who find themselves puzzled by a non-smiling situation like on a subway in Russia.
感情表現──笑顔も確かにその一部です──において、他の社会的要因がより大きな役割を果たしているかもしれない、ということを発見した他の研究もある点は注目に値します。さらに、いくつかの文化では幸福をあまり高く評価しないという証拠もあり、それが、その国の人々がどれくらいの頻度で無理に笑顔を作るかに影響しているかもしれません。クリスの研究はさらに拡大し、繰り返すこともできるでしょうが、それはきっと、ロシアの地下鉄のような笑顔のない状況に戸惑う、幸せなアメリカ人を安心させてくれることでしょう。
ポイント｜[7]-[9]＝問11・12
実験（[7]）と結果（[8]）：独・スイス＝笑顔は知性が高い／印・イラン・露＝知性が低い。インド（低）をスイス（高）と比べると「より低い」（問11）。同じ笑顔が国で正反対に評価される＝perception varies from country to country（問12）。
