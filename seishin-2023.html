1I noticed recently that someone posted on Twitter about Japanese having no Adistinction between "he" and "she" until the late 19th century, when the word kanojo was Binvented in order to translate the word "she" from European languages. "Before that," the person wrote, "kare was used for everyone, regardless of gender."

2In recent years, an obsession with the gender of pronouns has become a prominent feature of "culture wars" in the West. Looking at the reaction to this tweet, I saw many shocked comments on Twitter about how Europeans had imposed gendered pronouns on the Japanese.

3The tweet was slightly misleading, though raises an interesting subject — how different languages handle gender differences. It's not so much that the Japanese did not differentiate between the sexes before the late 19th century, but the manner in which they have done so has changed over the centuries. In written English, and most European languages, every single sentence constantly reminds you of the gender of a person through the use of male or female pronouns. In contrast, Japanese, both spoken and written, can get by with far less use of pronouns, and indeed feels no need to indicate the gender of a person in any number of sentences describing them.

4Until the Meiji period (1868–1912), when the Japanese language began to import many elements of grammar from English, there was no third person pronoun in Japanese at all.

5Faced with the problem of how to translate such things as European novels into Japanese, after a considerable amount of head-scratching and experimentation, the word kare ("over there") was adopted to fulfil this role, and at first, for a brief period, was equivalent to both "he" and "she."

6In the Meiji era, if a Japanese writer wanted to specify that the third person was female then they would simply tag on to the character for kare the character for "woman," which was pronounced ka no onna ("she"). Only in the Taisho period (1912–1926) did the pronunciation of this word settle on the modern form, kanojo.

7All of this makes pronouns in Japanese in the late nineteenth century and early twentieth century Cconfusing.

8For example, when in his "Letter from London" of 1901, the writer Natsume Soseki wrote about his workman-like maid in a London boarding house, he refers to her repeatedly as kare, making me wonder — when I translated the story for publication 20 years ago — whether Soseki was deliberately, and perhaps Dcruelly, highlighting her lack of womanly charm.

9It is more likely, however, to have been Esimply a late example of a rapidly changing linguistic landscape in which kare could still refer to both men and women before modern gender-specific pronouns became firmly established. By the time Soseki started writing his novels in 1904, this gender-unspecific use of kare had dropped away.

10Over the following century, as more and more European and American novels were translated into Japanese and their influence absorbed, the use of pronouns in modern Japanese steadily increased.

11Compared to 60 or 70 years ago, when the practice was overwhelmingly common, women when speaking Japanese today show considerably less signs of "gender stylization", both in real life and on the literary page. The sentence-end particle wa for example has become increasingly rare and atashi has seemingly lost its vogue. In the old days you could read a written dialogue in Japanese and instantly tell the gender of the speakers. These days that's by no means true.

12In other words, over the last 150 years, some linguistic means of highlighting gender have gone slightly out of vogue in Japanese (stylization of speech), while other means (greater use of pronouns) have increased.

導入｜ツイートの紹介と訂正 [1]-[3]
I noticed recently that someone posted on Twitter about Japanese having no distinction between "he" and "she" until the late 19th century, when the word kanojo was invented in order to translate the word "she" from European languages. "Before that," the person wrote, "kare was used for everyone, regardless of gender."
19世紀後半、ヨーロッパの言語から「she」という語を翻訳するために「カノジョ」という言葉が発明されるまで、日本語には「he」と「she」の区別がなかったと、最近ある人がツイッターに投稿していることに気がつきました。「それ以前は」と、その人は書いています、「すべての人にカレが使われていた」のだと。
In recent years, an obsession with the gender of pronouns has become a prominent feature of "culture wars" in the West. Looking at the reaction to this tweet, I saw many shocked comments on Twitter about how Europeans had imposed gendered pronouns on the Japanese.
近年、欧米では代名詞の性別にこだわることが「文化戦争」の顕著な特色になっています。このツイートに対する反応を見ると、ヨーロッパ人が日本人に対して性別のある代名詞をいかに押しつけたかに衝撃を受けた、というコメントが多く見られました。
The tweet was slightly misleading, though raises an interesting subject — how different languages handle gender differences. It's not so much that the Japanese did not differentiate between the sexes before the late 19th century, but the manner in which they have done so has changed over the centuries. In written English, and most European languages, every single sentence constantly reminds you of the gender of a person through the use of male or female pronouns. In contrast, Japanese, both spoken and written, can get by with far less use of pronouns, and indeed feels no need to indicate the gender of a person in any number of sentences describing them.
このツイートは少し誤解を招くものでしたが、異なる言語が性差をどのように扱うかという興味深い話題を提起しています。日本人は、19世紀後半以前は男女の区別をしていなかったというよりも、その区別の仕方が数世紀にわたって変化してきたのです。英語の書き言葉や、ヨーロッパのほとんどの言語では、文章の一つ一つが、男性・女性代名詞の使用によって、常に人の性別を思い起こさせます。これに対して日本語は、話し言葉でも書き言葉でも、代名詞の使用がはるかに少なくてすみ、実際、人を説明する文章がいくつあっても、その人の性別を示す必要性を感じないのです。

歴史｜カレ／カノジョの変遷 [4]-[9]
Until the Meiji period (1868–1912), when the Japanese language began to import many elements of grammar from English, there was no third person pronoun in Japanese at all.
日本語が英語から多くの文法要素を取り入れるようになった明治時代（1868〜1912年）までは、日本語には三人称代名詞が全く存在しませんでした。
Faced with the problem of how to translate such things as European novels into Japanese, after a considerable amount of head-scratching and experimentation, the word kare ("over there") was adopted to fulfil this role, and at first, for a brief period, was equivalent to both "he" and "she."
ヨーロッパの小説などを日本語に訳すにはどうしたらよいかという問題に直面し、頭を悩ませながら試行錯誤を重ねた末、この役目を果たすものとして「カレ（向こう）」という言葉が採用され、当初は短い間、「he」と「she」の両方に相当する役割を果たしました。
In the Meiji era, if a Japanese writer wanted to specify that the third person was female then they would simply tag on to the character for kare the character for "woman," which was pronounced ka no onna ("she"). Only in the Taisho period (1912–1926) did the pronunciation of this word settle on the modern form, kanojo.
明治時代、日本の作家は、三人称が女性であることを明記したい場合、「カレ」の字に「女」の字を付け加えるだけで、それを「カノオンナ」（she）と発音していました。この語の発音が現代の形「カノジョ」に落ち着いたのは、ようやく大正時代（1912〜1926年）になってからのことでした。
All of this makes pronouns in Japanese in the late nineteenth century and early twentieth century confusing.
これらすべてのことが、19世紀末から20世紀初頭にかけての日本語の代名詞を、紛らわしいものにしています。
For example, when in his "Letter from London" of 1901, the writer Natsume Soseki wrote about his workman-like maid in a London boarding house, he refers to her repeatedly as kare, making me wonder — when I translated the story for publication 20 years ago — whether Soseki was deliberately, and perhaps cruelly, highlighting her lack of womanly charm.
例えば、1901年に発表した「ロンドン便り」の中で、作家の夏目漱石は、ロンドンの下宿で働く労働者風の女中について書いたとき、彼女を繰り返し「カレ」と呼んでいます。20年前にこの物語を翻訳出版したとき、私は、漱石が意図的に、そしておそらく残酷に、彼女の女性としての魅力のなさを強調しているのではないかと思ったものです。
It is more likely, however, to have been simply a late example of a rapidly changing linguistic landscape in which kare could still refer to both men and women before modern gender-specific pronouns became firmly established. By the time Soseki started writing his novels in 1904, this gender-unspecific use of kare had dropped away.
しかし、これは、近代的な性別を特定する代名詞が定着する前に、「カレ」がまだ男女の両方を指すことができた、急速に変化する言語的状況の後期の一例にすぎなかったと考えるほうがありそうです。漱石が1904年に小説を書き始めるころには、この性別にとらわれない「カレ」の使い方は廃れていました。
ポイント｜[4]-[9]＝問1・問2-1・問2-3
[4]「明治まで三人称代名詞なし」＝問2-1（ツイートが誤解を招く理由）。下線部(C)confusing・(D)cruelly・(E)simply＝問1。[9] It is more likely, however ＝漱石の例の再考（残酷ではなく、カレが男女両方を指せただけ）＝問2-3。
変化｜この150年 [10]-[12]
Over the following century, as more and more European and American novels were translated into Japanese and their influence absorbed, the use of pronouns in modern Japanese steadily increased.
それに続く1世紀の間に、欧米の小説がますます多く日本語に翻訳され、その影響が吸収されていくにつれて、現代日本語における代名詞の使用は着実に増えていきました。
Compared to 60 or 70 years ago, when the practice was overwhelmingly common, women when speaking Japanese today show considerably less signs of "gender stylization", both in real life and on the literary page. The sentence-end particle wa for example has become increasingly rare and atashi has seemingly lost its vogue. In the old days you could read a written dialogue in Japanese and instantly tell the gender of the speakers. These days that's by no means true.
それが圧倒的に一般的だった60年前、70年前に比べて、現在の女性が日本語を話すとき、実生活でも文学の世界でも、「性の定型化」の兆候はかなり少なくなっています。例えば、文末助詞の「ワ」はますます珍しくなり、「アタシ」ははやらなくなってしまったようです。昔は、日本語で書かれた会話を読めば、話し手の性別がすぐにわかったものでした。しかし、今では決してそうではありません。
In other words, over the last 150 years, some linguistic means of highlighting gender have gone slightly out of vogue in Japanese (stylization of speech), while other means (greater use of pronouns) have increased.
言い換えれば、この150年の間に、日本語で性別を強調する言語的手段のうち、あるもの（発話の定型化）は少し廃れ、一方で他の手段（代名詞の多用）は増えてきたのです。
