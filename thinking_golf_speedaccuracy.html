[1] A golfer has to go through ⑴a long list of steps when putting: judge the line of the ball, the grain of the turf, and the distance and angle to the hole; position the ball; align shoulders, hips, and feet to the left of the hole; prepare for backswing. What advice should a coach give to a golfer? How about "Take your time, concentrate on what you are doing, and don't get distracted by anything around you." That seems like clever consulting to some, and patently obvious to others; in any case, it is backed by research on ⑵the so-called speed-accuracy trade-off; the faster a task is performed, the less accurate it becomes. And in fact, when beginners are advised to take their time, concentrate on their movements, and focus their attention, they perform better. ⑶Should we give expert golfers the same advice?

[2] In an experiment, beginners and expert golfers were studied under two conditions: they had either only up to three seconds for each putt or all the time they wanted. Under ⑷time pressure, as mentioned, beginners performed worse and had fewer target hits. Yet surprisingly, experts hit the target more often when they had less time than when they had no time limit. In a second experiment, players were either instructed to pay attention to their swing or distracted by ⑸an unrelated, second task (counting tape-recorded tones). When they were asked to pay attention to their swing, as one might expect, beginners did better than when they were distracted. Yet with experts, it was again the opposite. When experts concentrated on their swings, their performance decreased; when experts' attention was distracted, their performance actually improved.

[3] How can we account for this apparent paradox? Expert motor skills are executed by unconscious parts of our brains, and conscious thinking about the sequence of behaviors interferes with performance. Setting a time limit is one method to make thinking about the swing difficult; providing a distracting task is ⑹another. Since our conscious attention can focus on only one thing at a time, it is fixed on the distracting task and cannot interfere with the swing.

コーチはゴルファーにどんなアドバイスをすべきだろうか？「時間をかけて、自分がやっていることに集中し、周りのことで気を散らしてはいけない」というのはどうだろう。
That seems like clever consulting to some, and patently obvious to others; in any case, it is backed by research on the so-called speed-accuracy trade-off; the faster a task is performed, the less accurate it becomes.
これは人によっては巧みなアドバイスに聞こえ、またある人には当たり前のことに聞こえる。いずれにせよ、これはいわゆる「速度・正確性のトレードオフ」に関する研究に裏付けられている。タスクを速く行えば行うほど、正確さが失われるのだ。
And in fact, when beginners are advised to take their time, concentrate on their movements, and focus their attention, they perform better.
そして実際、初心者は「時間をかけて、動作に集中し、注意を向けるよう」アドバイスされると、パフォーマンスが向上する。
Should we give expert golfers the same advice?
プロのゴルファーにも同じアドバイスを与えるべきだろうか？
第2段落 — 実験の結果（逆説の核心）
In an experiment, beginners and expert golfers were studied under two conditions: they had either only up to three seconds for each putt or all the time they wanted.
ある実験で、初心者とプロのゴルファーが2つの条件下で調査された。各パットに最大3秒しか与えられないか、好きなだけ時間を使えるかの2条件だ。
Under time pressure, as mentioned, beginners performed worse and had fewer target hits. Yet surprisingly, experts hit the target more often when they had less time than when they had no time limit.
時間制約のある条件では、述べたように初心者のパフォーマンスは低下しターゲットへの的中数も減った。しかし驚くべきことに、プロは時間制限がある方が制限がない時より的中率が高かった。
In a second experiment, players were either instructed to pay attention to their swing or distracted by an unrelated, second task (counting tape-recorded tones).
第2の実験では、プレイヤーはスイングに注意を向けるよう指示されるか、無関係な第2のタスク（録音されたトーンの数を数えること）によって気をそらされるかのいずれかだった。
When they were asked to pay attention to their swing, as one might expect, beginners did better than when they were distracted. Yet with experts, it was again the opposite.
スイングに注意を向けるよう求められた場合、予想通り初心者は気が散っていた時より成績が良かった。しかしプロの場合、再び逆の結果となった。
When experts concentrated on their swings, their performance decreased; when experts' attention was distracted, their performance actually improved.
プロがスイングに集中したとき、パフォーマンスは低下した。プロの注意が散漫になったとき、パフォーマンスはむしろ向上した。
第3段落 — 逆説の説明（無意識のスキル）
How can we account for this apparent paradox?
この一見矛盾しているように見えるパラドックスをどう説明できるだろうか？
Expert motor skills are executed by unconscious parts of our brains, and conscious thinking about the sequence of behaviors interferes with performance.
専門家の運動スキルは脳の無意識の部分によって実行されており、行動の順序についての意識的な思考はパフォーマンスを妨げてしまう。
Setting a time limit is one method to make thinking about the swing difficult; providing a distracting task is another.
時間制限を設けることはスイングについて考えにくくする一つの方法であり、気を散らすタスクを与えることはもう一つの方法だ。
Since our conscious attention can focus on only one thing at a time, it is fixed on the distracting task and cannot interfere with the swing.
私たちの意識的な注意は一度に一つのことにしか集中できないため、気を散らすタスクにそれが固定され、スイングを妨害できなくなるのだ。
この問題から学ぶ — 「実験の構造を頭で動かす力」
