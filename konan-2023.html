本文｜PASSAGE

1Houseplants aren't just a way to make your home look better — according to research published this week, some of the most popular pot plants can also have a positive impact on the quality of the air.

2(1)Research from the Royal Horticultural Society (RHS) found that in a small, poorly air-conditioned office measuring about 15 cubic metres, just five small plants reduced levels of the common air pollutant nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) by as much as 20%. Exposure to high concentrations of NO₂, which gets into the air when fossil fuels are burned, can worsen lung diseases, particularly asthma. The researchers tested low-maintenance, inexpensive houseplants commonly found in UK homes, by putting them into test chambers containing levels of NO₂ comparable to those in an office next to a busy road. An hour later, each species of plant had removed about half the NO₂ in its chamber.

3The results are good news for the (2)green fingered, but 3 you're one of those people who find it difficult to keep anything leafy alive? Investing in plants can seem like a waste of money and time, while watching a living thing slowly die in front of you does not make for a cheerful hobby. Experts such as Jane Perrone, who presents the houseplant video podcast On the Ledge, say it doesn't have to be this way. "Although the lily will be weakened dramatically if it doesn't have enough water, houseplants in general are all easy to look after," she says.

4Perrone says lots of people buy their houseplants from supermarkets or DIY stores, as (4)they tend to be cheapest there. But this is a (5)false economy, she says, because you could end up with a plant that hasn't been looked after particularly well and wasn't worth buying. "If a plant has been sitting around for a while, sometimes it could be already stressed and damaged, so when you get it home, it starts to show signs of that stress. 6, it is best to buy from a more specialist grower, such as a houseplant shop or a garden centre, where there's actually somebody who's paid to look after the houseplants."

5(7)Online specialist plant shops that provide lots of detailed information about what they are selling and their living requirements are also worth considering, although a lot is dependent on the packaging and the home-delivery company which the website uses. "(イ)When choosing a plant in person, avoid those which have yellow or brown leaves and briefly check its roots," the RHS horticulture expert Leigh Hunt says. "If it's been overwatered, those roots will be decayed and brown." It (8)pays to think about where you are going to put your plant before you buy one, so that you can choose one that is well suited to the environmental conditions of that location. Perrone says many people who struggle to keep their plants alive often massively underestimate how much light all plants need. She recommends going to a specialist shop, where staff should be able to help you find something that will suit the conditions of your chosen location. "The plant may cost you a bit more but hopefully it means you won't go home with (9)the wrong one," she says.

6When you first take a plant home (10)bear in mind that even if it is a variety that can grow steadily in the shade it may 11 at first in a dark spot if it has become accustomed to sunnier conditions. "Don't think you can bring a plant home, put it on a shelf and not look at it for a month. If you're new to houseplants, (12)observation is really key," Perrone says.

7Too much watering — or too little — are problems that many (13)houseplant killers have to overcome. As Hunt says, "People often ask 'how many times a week should I water?'. And it's really impossible to say because every plant is different, it grows at different rates, in different temperatures, light conditions and seasons. My general advice to people is push your finger into the soil. If that feels moist, then don't water it. If it starts to feel slightly dry, then water it well." You may need to move your plants around in different seasons or put them in larger pots as they grow, so (ロ)while it's worth investing in the plant itself, you should avoid spending a fortune on beautiful pots.

② How come S+V?＝「どうして〜なのか？」（理由。語順がふつうの疑問文と違い S+V のまま）

7Too much watering — or too little — are problems that many houseplant killers have to overcome. As Hunt says, "People often ask 'how many times a week should I water?'. And it's really impossible to say because every plant is different, it grows at different rates, in different temperatures, light conditions and seasons. My general advice to people is push your finger into the soil. If that feels moist, then don't water it. If it starts to feel slightly dry, then water it well." You may need to move your plants around in different seasons or put them in larger pots as they grow, so while it's worth investing in the plant itself, you should avoid spending a fortune on beautiful pots.
